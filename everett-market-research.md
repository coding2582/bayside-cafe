## 1. The Everett Market — What the Data Actually Says

### 1.1 Size and money

| Metric | Value | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Population (2026) | ~113,900 | Big enough for volume, small enough that word-of-mouth travels fast |
| Growth rate | ~0.34%/yr (2.86% since 2020) | Slow-and-steady, not a boomtown — you win by taking share, not riding a wave |
| Median household income (2024) | $83,512 | Solidly middle-class; businesses can afford $300–$1,500/mo services |
| Per capita income | $56,314 | Enough disposable income for consumer-facing plays too |
| County | Snohomish County (~840K people) | **Your real market is the county, not just the city** |

**Analyst note:** Everett proper is ~114K, but your serviceable market is Snohomish County (Everett, Marysville, Lynnwood, Edmonds, Mukilteo, Lake Stevens, Snohomish, Mill Creek, Monroe, Arlington). That's ~840K people and tens of thousands of small businesses. Nearly every service in this report should be sold "Everett + 20-minute radius," not "Everett only." This roughly 7x's your addressable market.

### 1.2 The employment base (who your customers are)

Top employment sectors for Everett residents:

1. **Manufacturing** (~8,300) — dominated by **Boeing Everett** (~30,000 workers on-site, largest building in the world by volume) plus a dense aerospace supply chain (Onamac Industries, Cannon Aircraft Interiors, B/E Aerospace, etc.)
2. **Health Care & Social Assistance** (~8,200) — Providence Regional Medical Center is a massive anchor; dozens of independent clinics, dental, chiro, PT, med-spa
3. **Retail Trade** (~7,200)

Plus: The **Port of Everett** (the largest public marina on the West Coast), **Naval Station Everett**, **Washington State Ferries**, and **Paine Field** (now a commercial passenger airport since 2019).

### 1.3 The two big structural facts you must understand

**FACT #1 — Manufacturing is shrinking; services are the future.**
Everett's manufacturing base **shed ~7,000 jobs between 2012 and 2022**, largely Boeing-related contraction. The city's own economic development strategy is explicitly about *diversification* — away from single-employer aerospace dependence and toward small business, services, tourism, and the waterfront. **Translation: the city government wants more small businesses to succeed, and there is active support/funding for it. That's tailwind for anyone selling services to small businesses.**

**FACT #2 — The waterfront is booming while downtown reinvents itself.**
The **Port of Everett's Waterfront Place** is a 1.5-million-sq-ft, 65-acre mixed-use development with a 2,300-slip marina. New restaurants and retail opened through 2025–2026 (Net Shed Fish Market & Kitchen, Rustic Cork Wine Bar, Tapped Public House with the largest waterfront rooftop deck in the county, Marina Azul, Menchie's, Alexa's Cafe). **Downtown Everett** (Colby Ave corridor) is simultaneously seeing new independents open (Sobar coffee, Revive Bowls & Smoothies). **Translation: there is a steady stream of brand-new businesses that need branding, websites, signage, menus, photography, and systems — from day one, with a budget, and no incumbent vendor relationship.** New businesses are the single easiest customer to sell to.

### 1.4 The Everett "digital maturity" gap (the whole opportunity in one sentence)

National benchmarks that hold true — and are *worse* in a mid-size industrial city like Everett:

- Only **~31%** of businesses have added booking/appointment/ordering links to their Google Business Profile.
- **Fewer than 5%** have Google's reservation feature turned on.
- Booking-enabled listings convert at **6.8% vs 4.1%** without — a ~66% lift that most owners are leaving on the table.
- Businesses that reply to reviews just 25% of the time average **~35% more revenue**.
- The local **"3-pack"** (top 3 map results) captures **~44%** of all local search clicks — and most Everett SMBs are not in it.

**The thesis in one line:** *Everett is an industrial, blue-collar, family-business town where a large share of owners are excellent at their trade and behind on the internet. The gap between "how good these businesses are" and "how they look online / how they run their back office" is the opportunity.*

## 2. The Core Thesis — Where the Money Actually Is

Cutting through all 37+ ideas, they cluster into five strategic buckets. Ranked by attractiveness for *your* profile (solo, <$10K, sales-strong, wants recurring revenue):

| Rank | Bucket | Why it wins | Why it's risky |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Recurring digital services** (GBP optimization, local SEO, reputation/review management, managed websites, monthly marketing) | Monthly recurring revenue (MRR), near-zero cost of delivery, AI-augmented not AI-replaced, huge underserved base | Moderate competition; churn if you don't deliver results |
| **2** | **Automation & systems** (booking systems, follow-up automation, CRM setup, review-generation, digital forms, client portals) | Almost nobody local sells this; high perceived value; recurring; hard for a Boeing machinist-turned-owner to DIY | Requires you to actually learn the tools; longer sales cycle |
| **3** | **Physical print/signage** (signs, vinyl, vehicle magnets, stickers, apparel, business cards) | Tangible, easy to sell, every business needs it, fast cash | Real competitors exist (Vinyl Lab, Davis Sign); equipment cost; commoditized |
| **4** | **Content/media** (business photography, drone, video, virtual tours, social content) | High margin on your time, visual = easy to sell, pairs with #1 | Time-for-money, harder to make recurring, more competition |
| **5** | **Software/IT** (custom software, apps, IT support, cybersecurity, POS integration) | High CLV, sticky | Longest to first sale, hardest skill, IT support competes with established MSPs |

**The single highest-leverage play for you** is a hybrid: lead with a **cheap, tangible, fast-to-deliver product** (GBP optimization or a website, or even signage) to get the first "yes," then convert that customer to a **monthly recurring service** (managed website + local SEO + review management + booking/automation). That combination — easy front door, sticky back end — is the core recommendation of this report and drives the Top 10.

## 3. Scoring Framework

Every idea below is scored on the 13 dimensions you requested. Definitions:

- **Demand (1–10):** How badly Everett businesses need it (10 = they're bleeding money without it).
- **Competition (1–10):** How crowded (10 = saturated; **lower is better for you**).
- **Startup Cost:** Real equipment + software to begin.
- **Profit Margin:** Gross / Net for a solo operator.
- **Monthly Revenue Potential:** Realistic solo, aggressive execution — at 6 months / 1 year / 3 years.
- **Difficulty (1–10):** Skill + operational effort (10 = very hard).
- **Scalability:** Solo → agency → company path.
- **Recurring Revenue:** Can it be monthly?
- **AI Risk:** Odds AI meaningfully replaces it within 10 years.
- **Sales Difficulty (1–10):** How hard to close (10 = very hard).
- **Customer Lifetime Value (CLV):** Avg total $ per customer.
- **Time to First Sale:** Realistic.
- **Barrier to Entry:** How hard for a competitor to copy you.

**Overall Score (/100)** in Section 9 is a weighted composite (weights disclosed there).

## 4. Tier A — Recurring Digital Services (the core of the recommendation)

### 4.1 Google Business Profile (GBP) Optimization
- **Demand:** 9 — Most Everett SMBs have unclaimed, incomplete, or neglected profiles; the 3-pack drives ~44% of local clicks.
- **Competition:** 4 — National agencies target Everett via landing pages, but few *local* specialists doing hands-on GBP work.
- **Startup Cost:** **~$200.** Laptop (owned), a $30/mo local-SEO tool (BrightLocal/Whitespark), Canva Pro ($13/mo), business cards. That's it.
- **Profit Margin:** Gross ~95%, Net ~85%. It's your time.
- **Monthly Revenue:** 6mo: $2K–$4K · 1yr: $5K–$8K · 3yr: $12K–$20K (as it converts into retainers).
- **Difficulty:** 3 — Learnable in a weekend; results are real and provable.
- **Scalability:** Solo easily; small agency yes (hire VAs to do grunt work); large = harder without productizing.
- **Recurring:** **Yes** — sell as $150–$400/mo "GBP management" (posts, Q&A, review responses, photo updates).
- **AI Risk:** Low-Medium — AI helps *you* do it faster; owners still won't do it themselves.
- **Sales Difficulty:** 3 — **Easiest sale in this report.** You can literally show them their current profile vs. a competitor's on your phone.
- **CLV:** $2,000–$6,000 (one-time cleanup + 12–24mo retainer).
- **Time to First Sale:** **1–2 weeks.**
- **Barrier to Entry:** Low technically, but relationships + local reputation compound. Medium once you have case studies.
- **★ Verdict:** The best *front door* in Everett. Cheap, fast, provable, converts to recurring.

### 4.2 Local SEO Agency

- **Competition:** 6 — This is the most contested category (EZMarketing, Visualwebz, LaunchUX, Night Fox, Top City, WebCitz, Pixel Global all chase Everett). **But most are regional/national with generic location pages — not embedded locals.**
- **Startup Cost:** **~$300–$500** (Ahrefs/SE Ranking or BrightLocal ~$50–$100/mo, laptop).

## 11. Competitive Analysis (by category)

### Digital marketing / web / SEO (Tier A) — the contested space
- **Who's here:** EZMarketing, Visualwebz (since 2008), LaunchUX (national-award boutique), Night Fox, Top City Marketing, WebCitz, Pixel Global IT, Seattle Website Developer.
- **Their pricing:** Websites $2,500–$10,000+ one-off; SEO/marketing retainers ~$750–$3,000/mo; national shops often $1,500+/mo minimums.
- **Their strengths:** Established, portfolios, teams, polished.
- **Their weaknesses:** **Most are regional/national running Everett *location landing pages* — not embedded locally.** Slow to respond, minimums price out the corner shop, treat Everett SMBs as small accounts, little face-to-face.
- **How you differentiate:** Be the *actual local* who meets them at their shop, answers texts same-day, starts at $150–$400/mo with no giant upfront, and shows provable local results. Own "the Everett guy."
- **Why customers switch:** Trust + responsiveness + price + being local. Small owners buy from people they can shake hands with.

### Signage / print (Tier C)
- **Who's here:** **Vinyl Lab NW** (Everett; HP Latex; wraps/signs/apparel/stickers), **Davis Sign Co.** (Everett; since 1988; wraps, large format, ADA), plus Bellevue Sign Co. and others servicing the area, Yelp "sign company" listings.
- **Their pricing:** Vehicle wraps $2,500–$5,000+; storefront signs $500–$5,000; banners $100–$300.
- **Their strengths:** Equipment, experience, install crews.
- **Their weaknesses:** Focused on bigger jobs; small/rush orders under-served; design-forward branding not their core.
- **How you differentiate:** Start as **design + broker** (no capital), obsess over turnaround and small orders, bundle signage into a *branding package* with the website/GBP work.
- **Why customers switch:** Speed, small-order friendliness, one vendor for brand + web + sign.

### Automation / systems (Tier B) — the open field
- **Who's here:** **Effectively no local specialists.** Owners buy tools (Calendly, Jobber, Housecall Pro, Podium, GHL) direct and self-implement poorly, or don't at all.
- **Their pricing:** SaaS $30–$300/mo, unconfigured.
- **Their weaknesses:** No local done-for-you setup, training, or ongoing management.
- **How you differentiate:** You are the *implementation + management* layer that doesn't exist locally. This is a near-empty competitive field — the report's biggest structural advantage.
- **Why customers switch:** They don't switch — they buy for the first time, from you.

### IT / MSP (Tier E)
- **Who's here:** Established regional MSPs serving clinics, offices, aerospace suppliers.
- **Their weaknesses:** Enterprise focus, high minimums, ignore micro-businesses.
- **How you differentiate:** Niche down (dental offices, small manufacturers) and undercut on responsiveness. Harder entry; later move.

## 12. Hidden Opportunities (things almost nobody in Everett offers)

These scored well precisely because **local competition is near zero** and the pain is real:

1. **Contractor/trades "lead-to-cash" automation** — auto-follow-up on quotes, review requests after every job, missed-call text-back. Everett is a *trades town*; this prints money and no one sells it. **(Top hidden pick.)**
2. **Missed-Call Text-Back** — a $99–$199/mo service that texts anyone whose call the business missed. Trivial to deliver via a GHL-type platform; ROI is undeniable (recovered jobs). Pure MRR wedge.
3. **Review-generation-as-a-service** — automated post-job/post-visit review asks. Directly tied to the ~35% revenue gap.
4. **AI receptionist / after-hours lead capture** for solo trades and clinics.
5. **Restaurant "de-DoorDash" package** — commission-free online ordering + QR + reviews for the waterfront/downtown restaurant boom.
6. **Digital forms + client portals** for contractors, law/accounting, med — kill the paper, look pro.
7. **Equipment / job tracking** for small industrial & construction firms — sticky, unserved.
8. **CMMC / cyber-compliance help** for Everett's defense-adjacent aerospace suppliers (Naval Station Everett + Boeing supply chain) — real regulatory driver, few local providers. (Skill-gated; later.)
9. **"New business launch" bundle** — sold to the constant stream of new waterfront/downtown openings: name→logo→website→GBP→signage→booking→reviews, one vendor, one price. Nobody packages this locally.
10. **Employee training portals** for high-turnover restaurants/gyms/trades — boring, sticky, recurring.

### "Boring businesses" with surprisingly high margins (adjacent, if you want a physical option)
- **Missed-call text-back / reactivation SaaS reselling** — ~90% margin, pure recurring.
- **Pressure washing / exterior cleaning** — <$5K to start (pressure washer + surface cleaner + trailer), 60–75% margins, huge Snohomish County residential/commercial demand, weak online competition, easy to bolt your *own* marketing skills onto. A genuine sub-$10K cash machine if you'd rather sell a service you can also market better than incumbents.
- **Junk removal / hauling** — truck + trailer, high margins, constant demand.

- **Portable restroom / dumpster rental** — boring, capital-heavier, near-annuity revenue.

These are included because they satisfy "high margin + low competition + sub-$10K," but they trade the *scalability and recurring software economics* of Tier A/B for physical labor. For a sales-strong 18-year-old who wants leverage, the digital plays win — but pressure washing is the strongest "physical fallback."

## 14. Your 90-Day Action Plan

**Days 1–14 — Set up (spend < $500).**
- Register the business (WA LLC ~$200) + free Google Workspace trial, Canva Pro, a GHL-type automation platform trial, BrightLocal.
- Build a 5-page website for *yourself* (prove you can do it) + a killer Google Business Profile for yourself (dogfood the pitch).
- Pick a niche to start: **home-service contractors** (Everett is a trades town, high job value, worst online presence, best fit for follow-up automation). Secondary: salons/detailers (booking) and downtown/waterfront restaurants (ordering/reviews).

**Days 15–45 — Land the first 3 clients (free/cheap to prove it).**
- Make a list of 100 Everett/Snohomish contractors with weak Google profiles (easy to find — just search).
- Offer the first 3 a **discounted or performance-based** GBP+Reviews setup to build case studies. Walk in, show them their profile vs. a competitor's, close on the spot.
- Deliver hard, document before/after (star count, ranking, calls).

**Days 46–90 — Convert to recurring + systematize.**
- Turn setups into $300–$500/mo management retainers.
- Add missed-call-text-back and follow-up automation as upsells.
- Ask every happy client for referrals (trades know trades).
- Target: **5–8 paying monthly clients ($2K–$4K MRR)** by day 90, on track to $10K MRR within the year.

**The core discipline:** Lead with the cheap, tangible, provable win (Google/reviews). Convert every win into monthly recurring revenue. Bundle upward. Stay local, stay responsive, out-hustle the national agencies who treat Everett as a landing page.
